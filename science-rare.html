RARE CONDITIONS AND DISORDERS

Harnessing deep genetic analysis to transform the lives of those facing uncommon illnesses

More than 7,000 distinct disorders are currently categorized as "rare" under the Orphan Drug Act, yet a staggering 95% of these remain without an FDA-approved therapeutic option. We are deeply committed to bridging this medical divide by engaging in fundamental scientific research to decode the complex genetic and biological drivers behind these conditions. Our rare disease initiatives intersect with nearly all of our core research divisions. The unifying factor across these diverse programs is the underlying genetic foundation of the illnesses, alongside the critically underserved, small patient populations they affect.

By integrating our proprietary discovery platforms with the massive datasets generated by our Genetics Center, we are steadily uncovering the core mechanisms of these diseases. This synergy has enabled us to pioneer a range of authorized and experimental treatments targeting rare and ultra-rare conditions through both advanced genetic and antibody-driven modalities.

Years of Rare Disease Research

Driven by data to uncover groundbreaking therapies for specialized conditions

Our foundational research philosophy applies equally to widespread global health crises and highly specialized patient populations. Our initial foray into experimental rare disease treatments focused on addressing specific elements of cryopyrin-associated periodic syndromes (CAPS)—a cluster of lifelong, recurrent disorders marked by chronic fatigue, joint pain, fevers, and rashes.

More recently, our pipeline has successfully yielded specialized therapies for homozygous familial hypercholesterolemia (HoFH), an ultra-rare genetic form of severe high cholesterol, as well as CHAPLE disease, a hereditary condition carrying life-threatening cardiovascular and gastrointestinal risks.

Current focal points in our rare disease pipeline include:

Fibrodysplasia ossificans progressiva (FOP) ▼

FOP is a profoundly debilitating and life-changing condition characterized by the progressive ossification (turning to bone) of muscles, tendons, ligaments, and connective tissues. Our research teams have dedicated over two decades to unraveling the complexities of FOP and its related pathways.

Driven by our proprietary breakthroughs regarding the mechanisms of this ultra-rare condition—which affects roughly 800 individuals globally—we are now advancing an innovative, internally discovered treatment candidate through late-stage clinical evaluation.

Learn about our FOP research
Lipodystrophy ▼

Lipodystrophy encompasses a group of rare metabolic disorders characterized by the abnormal loss or distribution of adipose (fat) tissue. This can lead to severe metabolic complications including insulin resistance, diabetes, hypertriglyceridemia, and hepatic steatosis.

Our research focuses on understanding the genetic basis of various lipodystrophy subtypes and developing targeted therapies to restore normal metabolic function and improve quality of life for affected patients.

Explore our lipodystrophy program
CHAPLE Disease ▼

CHAPLE (CD55 autoimmune hyperactivation) disease is an ultra-rare genetic disorder causing life-threatening protein-losing enteropathy, thrombosis, and immune dysregulation. Patients face severe gastrointestinal and cardiovascular complications.

We've developed a targeted therapy that addresses the underlying complement system overactivation, offering hope for this previously untreatable condition.

View CHAPLE clinical data
Homozygous Familial Hypercholesterolemia (HoFH) ▼

HoFH is an ultra-rare genetic form of severe high cholesterol affecting approximately 1 in 160,000 to 1 in 300,000 individuals worldwide. Patients experience extremely elevated LDL-C levels from birth, leading to premature cardiovascular disease.

Our specialized therapies target the genetic mechanisms underlying this condition, offering new treatment options for patients who don't respond adequately to standard cholesterol-lowering medications.
